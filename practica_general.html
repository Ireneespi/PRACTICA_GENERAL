Imperial College’s rodents: a little description

Author

Irene Espinosa Manjón

Context

The data used in this small study were collected by Dr. Malo and his students at Imperial College in July 2022. By trapping rodents at Silwood Park in tough field campaings, they obtained valuable data of many measurements, but here I will focus on the following: sex, age, anogenital distance, body weight and, the most difficult to achieve, basal metabolic rate.

Analysis and results

Species, sexes and ages distribution

These data include two different species, with 31 observations of the wood mouse (Apodemus sylvaticus) and 43 of the red vole (Myodes glareolus). As it is shown below in Figure 1, on the one hand, we have 31 females observations, 15 of A.sylvaticus (As) and 16 of M.glareolus (Mg). We also have 43 males observations, 16 of A.s. and 27 of M.g. On the other hand, there are 16 adult (A), 8 subadult (SA) and 7 juvenile (J) A.s. observations. M.g. has 30 adults, 7 subadults and 30 juveniles.

Biometric measurements

It seems that adult females of A.s. have the highest weight values on average (Table 1; Figure 2) and, in addition, adult males of A.s. show the highest anogenital distances (Figure 3). These results could be related to reproductive status, which I will not discuss here but would be interesting to explore. On the other side, juvenile males of A.s seem to be the most active or stressed and, on the contrary, juvenile males of M.g. are the least active, always comparing measurements taken in a non-breeding, post-absorptive and resting, within a thermoneutral zone. It could be a consequence of one outlier that increases the juvenile A.s. average (3.8693349, Table 1; Figure 4) and, also, because of the limited data for some categories, as bmr would be expected to be higher for those smaller individuals (juveniles).
